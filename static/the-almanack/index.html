The study, conducted by researchers emanuelr and N Soma Sekhar and published on LessWrong, analysed thousands of papers from top-tier conferences such as ICLR, ICML, and NeurIPS spanning 2019 to 2026. Using a language model (DeepSeek V4 Flash), they classified over 55,000 papers to identify 2,328 focusing on various aspects of AI safety. The methodology involved assessing paper titles and abstracts, categorising them into areas such as interpretability, alignment training, and adversarial robustness, among others.

Why This Matters

The significance of this growth cannot be overstated. As AI systems increasingly influence critical aspects of society, ensuring their safe deployment is paramount. This trend reflects a growing acknowledgment of the risks associated with advanced AI, including potential misalignment with human values and other safety concerns. The increase also underscores an evolving recognition within the AI community of the ethical implications of their work.

How It Works

The team employed an innovative approach by using an LLM to classify papers, focusing on four main categories: AI safety, truthfulness and reliability, ethics and fairness, and general capabilities. Each identified safety paper was further classified into 17 subdomains. This method allowed for a nuanced understanding of the AI safety landscape, providing insights into which areas are gaining traction and which remain underexplored.

Mapping Brain Networks to Revolutionise PTSD Treatment

In a groundbreaking study, researchers have mapped brain stem networks, paving the way for innovative treatments that allow PTSD patients to regulate their stress responses. This breakthrough combines rodent and human brain mapping to enable real-time feedback therapies.

The Discovery

Conducted by researchers from the Netherlands Institute for Neuroscience (NIN) and Maastricht University Medical Centre, the study investigates the periaqueductal gray (PAG) – a key midbrain structure responsible for defensive behaviour regulation. By mapping circuitry in rodents and using ultra-high-field 7T fMRI in humans, scientists identified brain regions regulating the PAG, which are potential targets for neurofeedback therapies.

Why This Matters

The relevance of this research lies in addressing persistent hyper-arousal states in PTSD and anxiety disorder patients. The PAG, described as an "unmonitored engine" in these conditions, remains overactive due to the failure of natural regulatory mechanisms. By targeting upstream cortical and subcortical areas, treatments can be more effective, offering hope to those affected by chronic stress disorders.

How It Works

To achieve this, researchers use precise techniques like optogenetics and calcium imaging in mice to understand cellular connections that influence fear extinction and stress control. This data is translated into human applications through 7T fMRI, which offers unparalleled resolution, allowing scientists to examine the intricate brain structures involved. Real-time neurofeedback then enables patients to visualise and consciously alter their brain activity, teaching them to control stress responses effectively.

Implications and Future Directions

This study opens new avenues for PTSD treatment by integrating fundamental neuroscience with clinical research. The ability to locate precise brain circuits governing stress and apply this knowledge in therapeutic contexts could redefine mental health treatment. Furthermore, this research sets the stage for further exploration into stress regulation, potentially expanding applications to other neurological conditions.

This partnership accelerates the transition from research to clinical application.

The Bigger Picture

This research illustrates the power of interdisciplinary collaboration in neuroscience, demonstrating how animal models can inform human treatments.
